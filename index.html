you and the city

There are codes in Austin that shape your life. They can help or hurt you, cost you money or save you money, cause frustration or delight.

They shape more than just you.

These codes help or hurt our near and far neighbors, visitors, and the environment.

codes as in rules, not as in secrets

Zoning codes are city rules that tell developers what and how to build on land.

Zoning breaks land into pieces called districts.

Each district has rules that developers and citizens must follow.

It sounds simple, but the process is messy. There are winners and losers. Often, it's citizens like you who lose.

zoning strategies

single use zoning

Restricts a piece of land to one purpose such as a home or business.

goals

It aims to spread growth and separate buildings to reduce overcrowding and increase public health and property values.

consequences

Single use zoning protected people and property in an industrial economy. But for Austin's service and high-tech economy, it's rules are ineffective. Large areas of Austin remain zoned this way.

Its rules often lead to car-dependent communities with lengthy car trips, traffic, dangerous streets, few public places, and harm to the environment.

overlay zoning

Integrates special zoning rules on top of existing rules.

goals

Overlay zoning offers flexibilty from the strict rules of single use zoning to achieve city or community goals.

Often it protects sensitive areas such as a historic part of town, but has a variety of uses.

incentive zoning

A type of zoning overlay that adjusts current zoning rules to balance conflicting desires of a community and a developer.

Zone before overlay.

Development before overlay.

goals

Incentive zoning encourages a trade-off between a developer’s desire for profit and a community’s desire for benefits such as parks and affordable homes.

inclusionary zoning

A type of zoning overlay that adjusts current zoning rules to include affordable homes for all people.

The subsidized cost, costs the developer not taxpayer'smoney to keep the houses affordable. but it does offer density and creative bonuses for developers.

goals

Inclusionary zoning arms to support a balanced community for all incomes and homes for employees of businesses located in a community.

cluster zoning

A type of zoning overlay that allows a developer to group buildings and structures together.

Goals

Cluster zoning aims to save land for parks and social gatherings, reduce pollution, lower development costs.

Mixed Use Zoning

Allows for multiple uses for a piece of land such as combining homes and offices in the same building.

Goals

It aims to reduce car dependency, traffic, pollution, foster community, provide a wider range of work and housing options.

Mixed use development expands housing opportunities, encourages pedestrian activity and an active lifestyle, promotes economic and social diversity, and preserves open space. FBCs can also help reduce auto-dependency, which can have positive environmental impacts by reducing the number of vehicle miles traveled and the associated air pollutants.

Beyond Zoning

As great as some of these methods have been at breaking from the harmful patterns of single-use zoning, they focus on use rather than form.

As a result they create unpredictable forms of development.

Planned Unit Development

Rezoned property to permit special land uses and forms.

Goals

PUDs aim to create a specific plan for a specific area for a wide variety of purposes.

Form-based Code

Goals

Form-based code is less concerned with use and more concerned with form. It ensures its buildings have consistent character, human scale, and compact development.

Change the City

Zoning cannot fully control the form of the city. It does however establish a foundation for developers to come in and build on the land. The complete built environment is a negotiation between these two forces.
